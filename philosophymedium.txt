## Section 1: Symbolic Logic and Formal Reasoning

1. Symbolic Logic and Formal Reasoning (Medium)
1. In the proposition "If P then Q," if P is false, the compound proposition is:
A) Always false
B) Always true
C) Depends on Q
D) Indeterminate
Answer: B

2. Which of the following represents the logical form of "All philosophers are wise"?
A) ∃x(Px → Wx)
B) ∀x(Px → Wx)
C) ∀x(Px ∧ Wx)
D) ∃x(Px ∧ Wx)
Answer: B

3. A tautology is a proposition that is:
A) Always false
B) Sometimes true
C) Always true
D) Contradictory
Answer: C

4. In a valid argument, if the premises are true, then:
A) The conclusion might be false
B) The conclusion must be true
C) The premises become false
D) Nothing can be determined
Answer: B

5. The statement "P ∨ ¬P" is an example of:
A) Contradiction
B) Contingency
C) Tautology
D) Fallacy
Answer: C

6. Which rule of inference allows us to conclude Q from "P → Q" and "P"?
A) Modus Tollens
B) Modus Ponens
C) Hypothetical Syllogism
D) Disjunctive Syllogism
Answer: B

7. The logical equivalence of "¬(P ∧ Q)" is:
A) ¬P ∧ ¬Q
B) ¬P ∨ ¬Q
C) P ∨ Q
D) P → Q
Answer: B

8. A syllogism with the form "All M are P, All S are M, therefore All S are P" is:
A) Invalid
B) Valid (Barbara)
C) Fallacious
D) Incomplete
Answer: B

9. The difference between validity and soundness is:
A) Validity concerns form, soundness concerns form and truth
B) They are identical
C) Soundness only concerns form
D) Validity only concerns truth
Answer: A

10. In categorical logic, a universal negative proposition is represented as:
A) All S are P
B) No S are P
C) Some S are P
D) Some S are not P
Answer: B
