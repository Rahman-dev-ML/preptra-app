# 2001-2005

### **Underlying Patterns and Repeating Questions in International Law Exams (2001--2005)**

Below are recurring themes, topics, and questions across the past
papers, grouped by subject area with years specified:

#### **1. Core Concepts of International Law**

1.  **Definition and Nature of International Law***2005 Q1*: \"Define
    International Law. Do you agree with John Austin's concept\...?\"

2.  *2001 Q1*: \"Object, purpose, and basis of International Law.\"

3.  **Sources of International Law***2005 Q2*: \"Sources of
    International Law. Discuss any two.\"

4.  *2003 Q7*: \"Steps in the creation of legal obligation by a treaty
    (Starke).\"

5.  **Subjects of International Law***2005 Q4*: \"Individuals as
    subjects of International Law.\"

6.  *2001 Q8(1)*: MCQ on \"subjects of International Law (States vs.
    individuals).\"

#### **2. State Sovereignty and Jurisdiction**

1.  **State Jurisdiction***2003 Q2*: \"State Jurisdiction\" (maritime
    belt, ports, foreigners).

2.  *2001 Q3*: \"Jurisdiction of ICL depends on consent of parties.\"

3.  **State Immunity***2002 Q4*: Debate on whether \"State Immunity is
    withering away.\"

4.  *2005 Q17*: MCQ on diplomatic immunity (civil vs. criminal).

5.  **Recognition of States/Governments***2003 Q4*: \"De facto vs. De
    jure recognition.\"

6.  *2005 Q14*: MCQ on \"Recognition of new States as a matter of
    policy.\"

#### **3. Law of the Sea**

1.  **Territorial Waters, EEZ, Continental Shelf***2001 Q5*: Short notes
    on internal waters, continental shelf, EEZ.

2.  *2005 Q18*: MCQ on \"territorial sea control.\"

3.  Repeated MCQs on Pakistan's maritime zones (12 nautical miles for
    territorial waters; 200 nautical miles for EEZ).

4.  **Maritime Doctrines/Cases***2003 Q11*: \"Floating Island Theory.\"

5.  *2005 Q20*: \"Byrkershoek principle\" (maritime belt measurement).

#### **4. Treaties**

1.  **Validity and Termination***2002 Q3*: \"Coercion invalidates
    treaties + other methods.\"

2.  *2001 Q20*: MCQ on \"vital change of circumstances (*rebus sic
    stantibus*).\"

3.  **Key Principles***2005 Q3*: \"*Pacta Sunt Servanda*\" (repeated in
    2002, 2005).

4.  *2001 Q19*: MCQ on \"Harmon Doctrine.\"

#### **5. International Dispute Resolution**

1.  **Amicable Means***2004 Q4*: \"Peaceful settlement of disputes.\"

2.  *2005 Q5*: \"Amicable means for dispute settlement.\"

3.  **Role of ICJ***2001 Q8(4)*: MCQ on ICJ judges' election.

4.  *2004 Q7*: \"Corfu Channel Case principles.\"

#### **6. Human Rights and Humanitarian Law**

1.  **Universal Declaration of Human Rights**Repeated MCQs across years
    (correct answer: 1948).

2.  **Prisoners of War***2004 Q5*: \"Treatment of POWs under
    conventions.\"

3.  *2005 Q7b*: \"Rights of Prisoners of War.\"

#### **7. Neutrality and Neutralized States**

1.  *2001 Q7*: \"Define Neutral and Neutralised States.\"

2.  *2005 Q6*: \"Rights and duties of neutral States.\"

#### **8. Key Doctrines and Cases**

1.  **Doctrines***Monroe Doctrine* (2002 Q5b, 2005 Q20).

2.  *Estrada Doctrine* (2003 Q7, 2004 Q3).

3.  **Landmark Cases***Lotus Case* (2001 Q2a).

4.  *Corfu Channel Case* (2004 Q7).

#### **9. Diplomatic and Consular Relations**

1.  **Immunities***2003 Q1*: \"Privileges and immunities of diplomatic
    agents.\"

2.  *2005 Q17*: MCQ on diplomatic immunity.

3.  **Establishing Relations***2001 Q7*: MCQ on \"diplomatic relations
    by mutual agreement.\"

#### **10. Compulsory MCQs (Yearly Repeats)**

1.  **UN Structure**:

2.  General Assembly vs. Security Council roles (2001 Q8(2), 2005 Q12).

3.  **ICJ**:

4.  Judges' election (2001 Q8(4)) and term (2004 Q8(1)).

5.  **Maritime Zones**:

6.  Territorial waters (12 nm), EEZ (200 nm) (2001--2005).

7.  **Key Dates**:

8.  Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), Vienna Convention
    (1961).

### **Most Frequently Tested Topics**

1.  **Law of the Sea** (Maritime zones, doctrines).

2.  **Treaties** (Validity, *pacta sunt servanda*).

3.  **State Sovereignty** (Jurisdiction, immunity).

4.  **International Courts** (ICJ structure, landmark cases).

5.  **Humanitarian Law** (POWs, human rights conventions).

    treaties).

#### **IV. Strategic Insights**

    **Geneva 1949**).

2.  **Essay Preparation**:

3.  Master comparative analyses (e.g., **custom vs. treaty**).

4.  Link theoretical principles to modern challenges (e.g., **cyber
    warfare**, **climate change**).

5.  Critically evaluate UN mechanisms (e.g., **veto power**, **Security
    Council reform**).

**Repeat Questions to Prioritize**:

1.  Recognition theories (2010, 2012, 2013).

2.  ICJ structure and precedents (2009, 2011, 2013).

3.  UNCLOS-III significance (2013).

4.  Role of non-state actors (2010, 2013).

# 2014-2018

### **Underlying Patterns and Repeating Questions in International Law Exams (2014--2018)**

**Note:** Topics marked with ★ indicate recurring themes across multiple
years.

### **1. Sources of International Law ★★★**

1.  **Key Questions:2014 Q2:** Consent as the basis of international law
    and sources.

2.  **2015 Q3:** Sources per ICJ Statute Article 38(1).

3.  **2016 Q3:** Material sources (e.g., treaties, customs).

4.  **2017 Q3:** Primary vs. Subsidiary sources.

5.  **2018 Q2:** \"General principles\" vs. positivism.

**Pattern:**

1.  Focus on **Article 38(1) of the ICJ Statute** (treaties, customs,
    general principles).

2.  Debates around **consent vs. positivism** and the role of state
    practice.

### **2. State Recognition and Sovereignty ★★★**

1.  **Key Questions:2015 Q6:** State recognition and existence.

2.  **2016 Q7:** De jure vs. de facto recognition; constitutive vs.
    declaratory theories.

3.  **2017 Q7:** Self-determination and Montevideo Convention.

4.  **2018 Q4:** Recognition as \"fluid and inconsistent state
    practice\" (Starke).

**Pattern:**

1.  **Montevideo Convention criteria** (population, territory,
    government, capacity).

2.  Distinctions between **de jure/de facto recognition** and legal
    consequences.

### **3. United Nations and Security Council ★★★**

1.  **Key Questions:2014 Q6:** Security Council composition.

2.  **2017 Q4:** Good offices/mediation vs. Security Council's
    \"highhandedness.\"

3.  **2018 Q5:** Security Council powers under UN Charter Chapters VI
    and VII.

4.  **2018 Q6:** General Assembly vs. Security Council in dispute
    resolution.

5.  **2018 Q7:** Recurring issue of **Security Council composition
    reform**.

**Pattern:**

1.  **Chapters VI (peaceful resolution) and VII (enforcement)** of the
    UN Charter.

2.  Critiques of **veto power** and demands for equitable
    representation.

### **4. Treaties and Conventions ★★**

1.  **Key Questions:2015 Q5:** Formation, interpretation, and
    termination of treaties.

2.  **2017 Q6:** Vienna Convention on treaties (definition, features,
    termination).

3.  **2018 Q8:** UN's role despite imperfections (linked to treaty-based
    systems).

**Pattern:**

1.  **Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969)** as a central
    reference.

2.  Focus on **pacta sunt servanda** and grounds for termination (e.g.,
    material breach).

### **5. Jurisdiction and State Responsibilities ★★**

1.  **Key Questions:2014 Q3:** Territorial sea limits and principles
    (acquiescence/opposability).

2.  **2016 Q4:** Freedom of High Seas vs. coastal state rights (hot
    pursuit).

3.  **2016 Q5:** Types of state jurisdiction (territorial, nationality,
    protective).

4.  **2015 Q4:** Diplomatic immunities vs. privileges.

**Pattern:**

1.  **UNCLOS (1982)** principles (territorial sea, EEZ, hot pursuit).

2.  **Jurisdictional conflicts** (e.g., Lotus Case, 2016 Q8a).

### **6. Theoretical Debates ★★**

1.  **Key Questions:2015 Q2:** Natural Law vs. Positive Law theories.

2.  **2017 Q2:** \"International Law is oxymoronic\" (effectiveness
    debate).

3.  **2018 Q2:** General principles vs. positivism.

**Pattern:**

1.  **Positivism vs. natural law** in shaping international norms.

2.  Critiques of IL's enforceability and relevance.

### **7. Case Studies and Short Notes ★★**

1.  **Recurring Cases:Corfu Channel Case** (2015 Q8a).

2.  **Lotus Case** (2016 Q8a).

3.  **Nottebohm Case** (2015 Q8c, 2016 Q7).

4.  **Rainbow Warrior Case** (2015 Q8b).

**Pattern:**

1.  Focus on **landmark ICJ cases** to illustrate principles (e.g.,
    state responsibility, recognition).

### **8. Specialized Topics (Single Occurrences)**

1.  **2014 Q8:** ILO as a model for organizations.

2.  **2017 Q5:** Treatment of POWs (Geneva Conventions).

3.  **2017 Q8c:** Collective use of force (UN Charter Article 42).

### **Summary of Key Recurring Themes**

1.  **Sources of IL** (every year).

2.  **UN Security Council** (composition, powers, reform).

3.  **State Recognition** (criteria, theories).

4.  **Treaty Law** (Vienna Convention).

5.  **Jurisdictional Principles** (territory, high seas, diplomatic
    immunities).

**Exam Strategy:**

1.  Master **Article 38(1) ICJ Statute** and **Vienna Convention**.

2.  Prepare critiques of **UNSC structure** and **state recognition
    theories**.

3.  Study key cases (e.g., Lotus, Nottebohm) and their legal principles.

# 2019-2024

### **Underlying Patterns and Repeating Questions in International Law Exams (2019--2024)**

Below are recurring themes and questions across the years, along with
the specific exam years and question numbers where they appeared:

#### **\*\*1. Monism vs. Dualism**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2019 (Q3)**: Distinguish between Monism and Dualism. Discuss
    incorporation/transformation.

3.  **2020 (Q3)**: Examine how states' adherence to monism/dualism
    shapes their approach to international law.

4.  **Pattern**: Focus on theoretical frameworks for domestic adoption
    of international law, modalities (incorporation/transformation), and
    practical implications for state practice.

#### **\*\*2. Origin and Nature of International Law**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2019 (Q2)**: Difficulty in historical accounts of international
    law's origin.

3.  **2022 (Q2)**: Historical account of origin and relevance in modern
    times.

4.  **2024 (Q5a)**: Debate on whether international law is \"law\" or
    \"positive morality.\"

5.  **Pattern**: Recurring focus on historical development, critiques of
    international law's legitimacy (Austin vs. modern enforceability),
    and relevance in contemporary contexts.

#### **\*\*3. Subjects of International Law (States vs. Non-State Entities)**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2022 (Q3)**: States as principal subjects; justification of
    individuals as subjects.

3.  **2023 (Q2)**: Influence of non-state entities on international law.

4.  **2024 (Q4)**: States as original subjects; advantages/disadvantages
    of their primacy.

5.  **Pattern**: Emphasis on states' legal personality, challenges posed
    by non-state actors (e.g., NGOs, armed groups), and evolving roles
    of individuals.

#### **\*\*4. Jurisdiction (Territorial, Prescriptive, Enforcement)**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2019 (Q4)**: Territorial jurisdiction over crimes.

3.  **2021 (Q4)**: Self-defense against non-state armed groups.

4.  **2024 (Q3a)**: Prescriptive jurisdiction types; **Q3b**:
    Enforcement jurisdiction (Lotus Case).

5.  **Pattern**: Questions on territorial sovereignty, enforcement
    jurisdiction (e.g., Lotus Case), and modern challenges like
    terrorism and cybercrimes.

#### **\*\*5. Use of Force and Self-Defense**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2019 (Q8)**: Article 2(4) prohibition and exceptions.

3.  **2023 (Q4)**: Customary law and Article 51 (terrorist attacks).

4.  **2024 (Q2a)**: Self-defense under Article 51 vs. customary law
    (Caroline incident).

5.  **Pattern**: Analysis of UN Charter provisions (Articles 2(4), 51),
    state practice, and debates on preemptive/anticipatory self-defense.

#### **\*\*6. International Court of Justice (ICJ)**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2021 (Q6)**: ICJ's jurisdiction and compulsory jurisdiction
    debate.

3.  **2022 (Q6)**: ICJ's structure, precedents, and role.

4.  **2024 (Q6)**: Advisory vs. contentious jurisdiction (Palestinian
    Wall opinion).

5.  **Pattern**: Focus on ICJ's jurisdiction types (consent-based vs.
    advisory), enforcement challenges, and landmark cases.

#### **\*\*7. State Responsibility and Reparation**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2019 (Q5)**: Imputability of insurrectional groups' conduct to
    states.

3.  **2023 (Q7)**: Constituent elements of state responsibility; Draft
    Code of 2001.

4.  **Pattern**: Emphasis on attribution of wrongful acts, forms of
    reparation (restitution, compensation), and state accountability.

#### **\*\*8. Recognition in International Law**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2022 (Q5)**: Constitutive vs. declaratory theories; consequences
    of non-recognition.

3.  **2024 (Q4a)**: Examples of states' legal personality (linked to
    recognition).

4.  **Pattern**: Theories of recognition, legal effects of
    recognition/non-recognition, and case studies (e.g., Kosovo,
    Taiwan).

#### **\*\*9. Treaties and Customary Law**

1.  **Years & Questions**:

2.  **2023 (Q5)**: Definition of treaties (Vienna Convention 1969).

3.  **2024 (Q7a)**: Importance of treaties; **Q7b)**: Treaty formation
    steps.

4.  **Pattern**: Focus on treaty formation (VCLT), *jus cogens*, and
    interaction between treaties and customary law.

#### **\*\*10. Short Notes on Recurring Topics**

1.  **Frequent Topics**:

2.  Diplomatic Immunity (2020 Q8b, 2021 Q8, 2024 Q8a).

3.  Asylum (Territorial/Extra-Territorial) (2022 Q8b, 2023 Q5ii).

4.  Cases (Nottebohm, Lotus) (2022 Q8c, 2024 Q3b).

5.  IHL Principles (Perfidy/Ruses, Blockade) (2020 Q5, 2024 Q8c).

### **Key Observations:**

1.  **High-Frequency Topics**:

2.  Monism/Dualism, ICJ jurisdiction, use of force, state
    responsibility, and subjects of international law.

3.  **Emerging Trends**:

4.  Increased focus on non-state actors (terrorist groups, NGOs) and
    modern challenges (cyber warfare, terrorism).

5.  **Case Law**:

6.  Repeated references to landmark cases: *Lotus Case*, *Caroline
    Incident*, *Nottebohm Case*, ICJ Advisory Opinions.

7.  **UN Charter Articles**:

8.  Articles 2(4), 51, and Chapter VII provisions are frequently tested.

**Study Priority**: Focus on foundational theories (Monism/Dualism),
ICJ, use of force, state responsibility, and recurring cases. Short
notes topics (diplomatic immunity, asylum) should also be thoroughly
prepared.
