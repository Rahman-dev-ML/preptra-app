## **4. Summary of Key Observed Patterns**

1.  **Structure of Exams\**

    - Each Paper I tends to split into two sections:

      - **Section A**: Abstract algebra and linear algebra (group
        theory, ring theory, vector spaces, transformations).

      - **Section B**: Coordinate geometry, 3D geometry, vector calculus
        topics (curves, surfaces, planes, distances, curvature).

    - Each Paper II also splits into two sections:

      - **Section A**: Real analysis (limits, continuity, derivatives,
        series, integrals, metric space topology).

      - **Section B**: Complex analysis (analytic functions, contour
        integrals, residues, De Moivre's theorem) and Fourier series.

2.  **Topics That Appear Every Year\**

    - *Group Theory*: Normal subgroups, factor groups, permutations,
      homomorphisms.

    - *Vector Spaces & Linear Operators*: Basis, dimension, eigenvalues,
      diagonalization.

    - *Real Analysis*: Convergence tests for infinite series, Riemann
      integrals, improper integrals.

    - *Complex Analysis*: Cauchy integral formula, De Moivre's theorem,
      expansions in partial fractions or Laurent series, singularities,
      residues.

3.  **Variants / Repeated Question Styles\**

    - Showing a finite integral domain is a field.

    - Applying the Cayley--Hamilton theorem to a 2×22\\times 2 or
      3×33\\times 3 matrix and then using that to find inverse or to
      diagonalize.

    - Checking whether a function f(z)f(z) is analytic by verifying the
      Cauchy--Riemann equations.

    - Using the residue theorem or Cauchy's integral formula to evaluate
      a contour integral around a circle.

    - Finding the curvature or evolute of a special curve (cycloid,
      cardioid, astroid).

    - Determining real integrals via Beta/Gamma function relationships.

4.  **Year-by-Year Similarities\**

    - **2009 & 2010** have very similar coverage of group theory and
      ring theory (especially normal subgroups and integral domains), as
      well as typical real-analysis topics like absolute convergence and
      Beta/Gamma integrals.

    - **2011 & 2012** again maintain the same algebraic core but also
      place emphasis on normalizers, index-2 subgroups, and aspects of
      geometry (2012 particularly highlights surfaces, conic sections,
      pedal equations, and parametric curves).

### **Concluding Notes**

If you are studying for these Pure Mathematics exams across different
years:

- **In Algebra (Paper I, Section A)**:\
  Emphasize normal subgroups, factor groups, and homomorphisms
  (especially isomorphism theorems). Practice working with rings
  Zn\\mathbb{Z}\_n, fields, and verifying whether a given ring is a
  field or has zero divisors. Master linear algebra fundamentals
  (rank--nullity, eigenvalues, diagonalization, Cayley--Hamilton).

- **In Geometry & Calculus (Paper I, Section B)**:\
  Practice finding arc length, curvature, tangents/normals, and evolutes
  for standard curves (astroid, cardioid, cycloid, conics), plus 3D
  topics (planes, lines, surfaces).

- **In Real Analysis (Paper II, Section A)**:\
  Get comfortable with definitions of limits, continuity, derivatives,
  Riemann integrals, improper integrals, convergence tests, Beta/Gamma
  functions, and expansions of standard functions.

- **In Complex Analysis & Fourier (Paper II, Section B)**:\
  Be ready to apply Cauchy's integral formula and residue calculations,
  classify singularities, handle expansions using De Moivre's theorem,
  and do standard Fourier series expansions on intervals like
  \[0,2π\]\[0,2\\pi\].

The same *families of questions* keep returning, often with only slight
variations of parameter values, or different functions/curves.
Consequently, a strong conceptual grasp of these recurring areas is the
key to tackling the exams year after year.

Below is an overview of how certain themes recur across these papers

1.  GROUP THEORY

**a. Center of a Group, Normal Subgroups, and Factor Groups**

- The concept of the *center* Z(G)Z(G) appears multiple times:

  - 2013, Q1(b): Shows Inn(G)≅G/Z(G)\\text{Inn}(G) \\cong G/Z(G).

  - 2013, Q2(b): Criterion for abelian groups in terms of G/Z(G)G/Z(G).

  - 2014, Q1(b): Proof that ZZ is a subgroup of GG.

  - 2015, Q1(b) and Q2(b): Various remarks about the center, normal
    subgroups, prime-order groups.

  - 2017, Q1(b): If N,MN,M are normal, then NM/M≅N/N∩MNM/M \\cong N/N
    \\cap M.

Repeatedly, students are asked to prove relationships between normal
subgroups, factor groups, and the group center.

**b. Groups of Prime Order or Order pqpq**

- *Groups of prime order are always cyclic*:

  - 2013, Q2(a) has a related idea about cyclic homomorphisms.

  - 2014, Q2(b) shows that any group of order 15 is cyclic.

  - 2015, Q1(b) re-mentions groups of prime order being cyclic.

**c. Abelian vs. Nonabelian Conditions**

- *Checking when a group is abelian:*

  - 2013, Q2(b): GG abelian if G/Z(G)G/Z(G) is cyclic.

  - 2014, Q1(a): If (ab)i=aibi(ab)\^i = a\^i b\^i for three consecutive
    integers ii, then GG is abelian.

**d. Homomorphisms, Kernels, and Isomorphisms**

- *Kernels are normal subgroups, isomorphism theorems, and homomorphism

  - 2013, Q2(a): Hom(A,B)\\mathrm{Hom}(A,B) for cyclic groups.

  - 2014, Q2(a): Kernel of ff is normal in GG.

  - 2015, Q2(b): GG is isomorphic to a subgroup of
    Aut(G)\\mathrm{Aut}(G).

  - 2016, Q2(a): Conjugation map Ia(g)=aga−1I_a(g) = aga\^{-1} is an
    automorphism.

Over and over, exams check knowledge of normality of kernels, the
significance of centers, and how factor groups or automorphism groups

SUMMARY OF REPEATING PATTERNS

1.  **Groups and their centers**: Many exams revisit the statement "GG
    is abelian iff G/Z(G)G/Z(G) is cyclic," or
    "Inn(G)≅G/Z(G)\\mathrm{Inn}(G)\\cong G/Z(G)."

    (or product of two distinct primes) order is cyclic."

    if nn is prime, plus identifying zero divisors in Zn\\mathbb{Z}\_n
    for composite nn. Also, the ring M2(R)\\mathrm{M}\_2(\\mathbb{R}) is
    a favorite example that is *not* commutative and *not* a field but
    *does* have a unity.

4.  **Subspaces, linear dependence, dimension**: Checking if a set of
    vectors is linearly dependent, or verifying dimension statements.

5.  **Characteristic polynomial and eigenvalues**: Determinant-based
    definitions and standard matrix examples appear every year.

6.  **Conic sections**: Ellipse standard forms, tangents, normals,
    parametric equations, and transformations to standard form are
    heavily repeated.

7.  **3D geometry**: Shortest distance between lines, equations of
    spheres, tangent planes to surfaces.

8.  **Fundamental calculus results**: Mean-Value Theorem,
    ε\\varepsilon-δ\\delta continuity, evaluating integrals to find
    areas/volumes.

9.  **Complex analysis**: Especially from 2016 onward, you see
    Cauchy-Riemann equations, contour integrals, Cauchy Integral
    Formula, and the Residue Theorem.
